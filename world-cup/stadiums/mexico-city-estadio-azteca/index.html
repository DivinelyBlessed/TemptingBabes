Estadio Azteca is the most historically significant football stadium in the world. Located in the south of Mexico City, it is the only venue ever to have hosted two FIFA World Cup finals — 1970 and 1986 — and it opens the 2026 tournament with the first match of the entire competition. During the tournament, FIFA refers to it as Mexico City Stadium in all official materials.
Why It Matters

The Cathedral of World Football

There is no stadium in the world with a deeper connection to the FIFA World Cup than Estadio Azteca. It hosted the 1970 final between Brazil and Italy — the match that cemented Pelé’s status as the greatest player of his generation. Sixteen years later, in 1986, it hosted Argentina vs West Germany in the final that gave Diego Maradona his World Cup winner’s medal. No other ground in football history carries that double legacy.

The 1986 tournament also gave Azteca two of football’s most debated moments: Maradona’s “Hand of God” goal against England in the quarterfinal, and his “Goal of the Century” — voted the greatest goal in World Cup history — were both scored on this pitch in the same match. Any football fan standing on the Azteca terrace in 2026 is standing on sacred ground.

  • Opening Match — June 11, 2026: Mexico vs South Africa kicks off the entire 2026 World Cup. The opening ceremony takes place here before a crowd of nearly 90,000.
  • Two World Cup Finals (1970 & 1986): No other stadium in history has hosted two World Cup finals. Azteca is the only one.
  • Maradona’s greatest moments: The “Hand of God” and “Goal of the Century” were both scored here in the 1986 quarterfinal against England.
  • Capacity of ~87,500: One of the largest football stadiums on earth, renovated ahead of 2026 to meet FIFA standards.
  • Host nation advantage: Mexico is one of three co-hosts in 2026. Playing the opening match at home, in front of this crowd, is an enormous advantage.

History

Estadio Azteca — The Full Story

Estadio Azteca opened in 1966 and immediately became the centrepiece of Mexican football. Constructed to host the 1970 FIFA World Cup, its debut tournament saw Brazil lift the Jules Rimet Trophy for the third time after defeating Italy 4–1 in the final. Brazil’s performance that summer — with Pelé, Jairzinho, and Gérson — is widely considered the greatest team display in World Cup history, and it happened here.

The stadium hosted the World Cup again in 1986 after Colombia withdrew from hosting duties. That tournament belonged to Diego Maradona, who produced two of the most discussed moments in football history within five minutes of the same quarterfinal match against England. First, the deliberate handball that he later described as scored “a little with the head of Maradona and a little with the hand of God.” Then, eleven seconds and eleven touches later, the goal later voted the greatest in World Cup history. Both on the Azteca pitch. Argentina went on to beat West Germany 3–2 in the final in the same stadium.

The ground underwent significant renovation ahead of 2026 to comply with FIFA’s infrastructure requirements. The work maintained the stadium’s original character while upgrading seating, media infrastructure, and access routes. It remains one of the largest football-specific stadiums anywhere on earth.

Logistics

Getting to Estadio Azteca

Estadio Azteca is located in the Tlalpan borough of southern Mexico City, approximately 14 km from the city centre. It is one of the most accessible major stadiums in Latin America by public transport, which is significant given that Mexico City traffic is consistently heavy — particularly during major events.

The metro is overwhelmingly the recommended option. Line 2 of the Mexico City Metro terminates at Tasqueña station, from where the Tren Ligero light rail line runs directly to Estadio Azteca station — a journey of under 10 minutes. Driving is possible but parking is extremely limited and prices will be significantly elevated for World Cup matches. For the Opening Match on June 11, plan to arrive well ahead of kick-off. FIFA recommends a minimum of two hours before major fixtures; for the opening ceremony, three hours is advisable.

  • Metro & Tren Ligero: Line 2 to Tasqueña, then Tren Ligero to Estadio Azteca station. Fast, cheap, and avoids all traffic.
  • Driving: Not recommended. Mexico City traffic is severe and World Cup match days will make it significantly worse. Parking is very limited.
  • Timing — Opening Match: Arrive by 12 noon ET for the opening ceremony. Kick-off is 3 PM ET / 7 PM UTC on June 11.
  • FIFA branding: The venue operates as “Mexico City Stadium” during the tournament. Check official FIFA venue maps for entry gate assignments.
  • Altitude: Mexico City sits at 2,240 metres above sea level. Visitors unused to altitude may feel the difference, especially in heat. Stay hydrated.

Two — more than any other stadium in history. The 1970 final (Brazil 4–1 Italy) and the 1986 final (Argentina 3–2 West Germany) were both played at Azteca. No other ground has hosted two World Cup finals.

What is the best way to get to Estadio Azteca?
The Mexico City Metro (Line 2 to Tasqueña) then the Tren Ligero light rail to Estadio Azteca station is by far the fastest and cheapest option. Driving is not recommended due to Mexico City traffic and severely limited parking on match days.
